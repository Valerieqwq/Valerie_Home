Music Sequential Memory Project

Project : Music Sequential Memory

About The Project :

Sequential memory is one of the most basic and crucial skills in human life. In this study, musical sequences were used as natural sequence materials to explore the neural mechanism of sequential memory processing. Here's the pilot study in which we use scalp EEG technology to investigate how prediction is formed through learning process and how it may shape representation of sequential memory.

Current State :

After we settled our experiment paradigm, we collected some scalp EEG data to do the primary ERP analysis. The result shows that with the increase of repetition times of learning the music pieces, there’s a significant Mismatch Negative (MMN) wave in the standard music piece compare to the deviant music piece. We also expected to see a tendency that the latency of MMN is getting shorter and the amplitude is getting stronger through the learning process. But due to the lack of trials, we can’t tell if there’s a such tendency.

Future work will be focused on solved the lack of trials problem and also try to improve the Signal noise ratio since the music itself will cause noises in brain activity. After that, we should calculate the coherence between music pieces and the EEG entrainment signal, and use the coherence to compare different stages of learning (from unfamiliar to familiar). We can also use the representation similarity analysis (RSA) to investigate if this can also proved the predictive coding (PC) theory.

Remember-Know Paradigm

Project : Remember-Know Paradigm

About The Project :

Successful memory retrieval is considered as a process of memory trace reproduction. The dual-process model of recognizing memory believes that the retrieval based on "recall" and "familiar" recognition is two different processes and there is separation in the brain region. Recent studies have pointed out that memory signals depend not only on the independent neural representation of the encoding or retrieval stage, but also on the interaction between the two, namely the encoding-retrieval similarity (ERS). Theoretical studies suggest that the reproduction of neural activation patterns only occurs in recollections, and that there is not enough evidence to support the "remember" of the activation of neural activation patterns and how does it differ from the reproduction of neural activation patterns induced by "memory"? Using the large sample EEG data and Representation Similarity Analysis (RSA), this study explored the above problems.
